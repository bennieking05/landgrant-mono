## IMPORTANT NOTICE

This document explains your rights as a property owner when a governmental entity or other condemning authority seeks to acquire your property through eminent domain. This information is required by Texas law (Texas Property Code Section 21.0112).

---

## YOUR FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS

### 1. Right to Adequate Compensation

Under the Texas Constitution (Article I, Section 17), you are entitled to receive **adequate compensation** for your property. This includes:

- **Fair Market Value** of the property taken
- **Damages to the Remainder** if only part of your property is taken (severance damages)
- **Relocation Assistance** if applicable under federal or state programs

### 2. Right to Notice

You have the right to receive:

- Written notice before any condemnation proceeding begins
- A copy of this Landowner's Bill of Rights **at least 7 days before** a final offer is made
- Written offers stating the amount offered and basis for that amount

### 3. Right to Negotiate

- You have **at least 30 days** to respond to an initial offer before a final offer can be made
- You have **at least 14 days** to consider any final offer before condemnation proceedings can begin
- You are **NOT required to accept any offer** and may negotiate for better terms

### 4. Right to an Appraisal

- You have the right to receive a copy of the appraisal on which any offer is based
- You may hire your own appraiser at your expense
- If condemnation proceeds to court, you may present your own appraisal evidence

### 5. Right to Legal Counsel

- You may hire an attorney to represent you at any stage of the process
- **Attorney's fees may be recoverable** if the condemning authority lacks the authority to condemn or fails to follow proper procedures

## THE CONDEMNATION PROCESS IN TEXAS

### Step 1: Initial Offer

The condemning authority must make a **written offer** to purchase your property based on an appraisal. You must be given at least **30 days** to respond.

### Step 2: Good Faith Negotiations

Texas law requires the condemning authority to negotiate in good faith and attempt to reach a voluntary agreement.

### Step 3: Final Offer

If negotiations fail, you will receive a **final written offer**. You must be given **at least 14 days** to accept or reject this offer.

### Step 4: Condemnation Petition

If you reject the final offer or do not respond, the condemning authority may file a petition in district court seeking to condemn your property.

### Step 5: Special Commissioners

The court will appoint **three special commissioners** (local landowners) who will:

- Inspect the property
- Hear evidence from both sides
- Determine the amount of adequate compensation

### Step 6: Objections and Trial

Either party may object to the commissioners' award:

- You have **20 days** to file objections after the award is filed
- If objections are filed, the case proceeds to trial
- You have the right to a **jury trial** to determine just compensation

### Step 7: Payment and Possession

- The condemning authority must pay the awarded amount before taking possession
- **Quick Take**: In some cases, the condemning authority may deposit the commissioners' award and take possession before the case is concluded

## YOUR RIGHT TO CHALLENGE

You may challenge the condemnation on several grounds:

### A. Public Use Challenge

You may argue that the taking is not for a legitimate public use. Under Texas law:

- Property **cannot be taken** for purely economic development purposes (SB 7b, 2005)
- Property **cannot be taken** primarily to increase tax revenue
- Property **cannot be transferred** to private parties except in limited circumstances

### B. Necessity Challenge

You may argue that the condemning authority does not actually need your specific property.

### C. Procedural Challenges

You may challenge the process if the condemning authority:

- Failed to provide proper notice
- Failed to negotiate in good faith
- Did not follow statutory timelines
