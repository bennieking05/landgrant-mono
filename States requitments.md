Eminent Domain Legal Requirements Across U.S. States

Regional Similarities and Differences

Northeast
	•	Procedural Initiation: Many Northeastern states require formal pre-condemnation procedures. For example, New York’s Eminent Domain Procedure Law mandates a public hearing with notice to affected owners, followed by published “Determination and Findings” before a taking ￼ ￼. Similarly, other states in the region (e.g. NJ, CT, PA) require public notice or hearings and a governmental resolution of necessity before filing a condemnation action. Negotiation for voluntary purchase is generally expected as a first step across the region, and an appraisal-based offer must often be made to the owner prior to litigation.
	•	Compensation Rules: Northeastern states uniformly adhere to “just compensation” at fair market value, including compensation for partial takings (severance damages to remaining property). Most do not provide enhanced multipliers on value; they focus on fair market value of the property and damages. Relocation assistance is provided when federal law requires (and some states have analogous state relocation statutes), but additional payments for business losses or attorney fees are uncommon in this region. For example, New York generally pays fair market value and severance damages; additional statutory allowances (attorney, appraisal fees) are only awarded if the final compensation far exceeds the condemnor’s offer ￼ ￼. New Jersey and Pennsylvania permit recovery of appraisal costs and limited fees in some cases, but not the broad coverage seen in some other regions.
	•	Owner Rights & Notice: Property owners in the Northeast have the right to challenge a taking’s validity (public use) within strict timeframes, though courts often defer to legislative determinations of public use. In New York, for instance, an owner can petition for judicial review of the agency’s “public use” finding within 30 days after the Determination and Findings ￼ ￼. Notice periods tend to be short – owners are typically served with a notice of intent or a condemnation petition not long after the final decision, with statutory timelines to respond or object (e.g. 30 days to appeal a public use determination in NY). The right to a jury trial for determining compensation is preserved in most Northeastern states (Pennsylvania and others allow jury or commissioner panels on compensation). However, appeals on compensation must follow specific procedures (e.g. PA uses a Board of Viewers’ award that either party can appeal to a jury trial).
	•	Permissible Takings (Public Use): Post-Kelo, Northeastern states show a mix of responses. Some strengthened protections against takings for private development: e.g. New Hampshire and Maine amended laws to bar eminent domain for economic development alone (NH’s constitution now prohibits takings if the primary purpose is private development or increased tax revenue ￼). Delaware enacted a 2005 reform barring takings for economic development except for true public use ￼. Pennsylvania and Connecticut tightened definitions of “blight” – eminent domain for redevelopment now requires a genuine blight designation of the property or area, not merely prospective economic benefit. By contrast, New York did not enact new limits post-Kelo and continues to allow takings for public purposes broadly defined (including redevelopment), relying on blight designations and public benefit findings under existing law. Overall, within the Northeast, there is a shared baseline that eminent domain can only be exercised for “public use,” but states vary in how strictly they limit “public use” to exclude predominantly private ventures. Many now explicitly disallow takings solely to boost economic development or tax bases ￼, bringing the region more in line with nationwide trends after 2005.

Midwest
	•	Procedural Initiation: Midwestern states generally require a clear statutory authorization and some form of preliminary procedure before condemnation. Most states in this region mandate a bona fide offer or good-faith negotiation with the owner as a prerequisite to filing a condemnation action ￼. For example, Michigan law requires that the condemning agency make a good-faith written offer based on an appraisal and, if the owner does not accept, file a judicial complaint in court ￼ ￼. Owners must be given notice of the impending action and have a limited window to contest the taking’s validity (Michigan owners have a “very limited time period” after service of the condemnation complaint to challenge the public use or necessity of the taking ￼). Several states in the Midwest use two-step proceedings: an initial administrative or court-appointed commission determines preliminary compensation, and either party can appeal that award to a court for a full trial. For instance, Missouri requires at least 60 days’ notice to the owner before filing, and a written final offer at least 30 days in advance ￼ ￼; if negotiations fail, commissioners are appointed to award value, with right of trial de novo afterward. Overall, procedural safeguards (notice and offer requirements) were bolstered across the Midwest post-2005 to ensure owners are informed and given a chance to sell without litigation.
	•	Compensation Rules: Midwestern states tend to provide robust compensation, in some cases exceeding simple market value. Many follow the principle of making the owner “whole” – including payment for the land taken and any decrease in value to the remainder of the property ￼. Notably, Michigan and Missouri law go beyond 100% fair market value in special situations: Michigan’s constitution guarantees 125% of fair market value for an owner-occupied principal residence taken ￼, and it allows business owners to recover business interruption losses or the going-concern value of a business if it cannot relocate ￼. Missouri law grants a bonus payment (often called “heritage value”) of 50% above market value for property held within the same family for 50+ years, and 25% above market for other homestead (owner-occupied) properties ￼. These enhancements reflect a regional trend to ensure extra compensation where takings are seen as particularly disruptive to long-term owners or residences. Additionally, many Midwest states provide for reimbursement of attorney fees and costs to owners who prevail or substantially improve on the government’s offer. For example, Michigan statute entitles owners to recover reasonable attorney fees, expert witness fees, and litigation costs from the condemning agency ￼, making the property owner financially whole (this is triggered regardless of outcome, as long as the taking occurs and compensation is due). Ohio, Indiana, Wisconsin, and others also award attorney fees in certain scenarios (often if the final award exceeds the initial offer by a defined percentage). The Midwest generally follows fair market value as the baseline for valuation (highest and best use standard), including value of improvements and severance damages to remaining land, but with these additional pro-owner measures in several states.
	•	Owner Rights & Notice: Owners in the Midwest have meaningful opportunities to be heard, though timing is crucial. Most states allow an owner to contest the right to take (public use or necessity) in the early phase of the case, either by a motion in the condemnation case or via a separate proceeding, but often under a tight deadline (e.g., Michigan owners must act shortly after the complaint is served ￼). Once that window closes or if the challenge fails, the taking will proceed. Owners universally have the right to a trial (often jury trial) on the amount of compensation. For instance, in Michigan either party may demand a jury for determining just compensation ￼, and in Missouri an owner unhappy with the commissioners’ award can appeal for a jury trial. Notice requirements in this region have become more stringent post-Kelo: several states require advance notice of intent to condemn well before a lawsuit. Missouri’s new law (2006) obligates condemners to give 60 days’ notice of intent and a written purchase offer held open for 30 days before filing ￼ ￼. Likewise, Indiana and Kansas require advance notice to property owners and often a public hearing or approval (e.g. Kansas now requires legislative approval for certain private-to-private takings). During negotiations, owners can obtain their own appraisals and often are entitled to reimbursement for appraisal costs. If a taking is deemed improper or is abandoned by the agency, Midwestern owners typically can recover litigation expenses. Overall, owners’ procedural rights to notice and to contest issues are well-recognized, but the burden is on owners to act quickly to assert those rights.
	•	Permissible Takings (Public Use): The Midwest saw significant reforms limiting the scope of “public use” after the Kelo decision. Virtually all Midwestern states now prohibit using eminent domain primarily for economic development or private commercial enterprise. Many enshrined this in statutes or even state constitutions. For example, Ohio’s Supreme Court and legislature declared that taking solely for economic benefit or private development is not a valid public use (a reaction to Kelo) ￼. Michigan amended its constitution in 2006 to state explicitly that “public use does not include the taking of private property for transfer to a private entity for the purpose of economic development or enhancement of tax revenues” ￼. States like Indiana and Wisconsin now allow takings for private redevelopment only if the property is blighted and meet stricter definitions of blight. Illinois and Minnesota similarly tightened their laws: Illinois requires blight to be proven by clear and convincing evidence for redevelopment condemnations, and Minnesota outlawed eminent domain for economic development except for parcel-specific blight remediation. In Missouri, the 2006 reforms bar takings “predominantly for private use” and require any use of eminent domain for redevelopment to have a true public purpose (with blight determinations meeting a higher bar) ￼ ￼. The common theme is that general economic improvement or increased tax base is no longer accepted as “public use” in the Midwest. Traditional public uses (roads, utilities, public buildings, etc.) remain valid, and genuinely blighted property can still be taken for redevelopment in many states, but even then often only on a parcel-by-parcel basis (to prevent abuse of broad “blight” designations). Midwestern legislatures also curtailed delegation of eminent domain to private entities except utilities. In summary, this region now offers some of the strongest post-Kelo property owner protections, sharply limiting eminent domain to true public ownership or use ￼, with notable exceptions carved out only for blight or public health and safety concerns.

South
	•	Procedural Initiation: Southern states generally impose rigorous procedural steps before eminent domain can be exercised, often outlined in “Landowner Bill of Rights” statutes or constitutional provisions. Texas, for example, has a detailed process: any entity with eminent domain authority must provide a Landowner’s Bill of Rights document to the owner (enumerating their rights) and make a bona fide purchase offer based on a professional appraisal ￼ ￼. The Texas Property Code requires an initial written offer, a 30-day waiting period, then a final offer, and at least 14 days for the owner to consider the final offer before any court petition is filed ￼ ￼. Similarly, Florida law mandates a good-faith presuit negotiation: the condemner must attempt to negotiate and cannot file suit until a reasonable offer is made and rejected ￼ ￼. If negotiations fail, Florida and Texas often employ “quick-take” proceedings – the agency files a petition and may deposit a good-faith estimate of compensation to quickly take title/possession, upon a court order, before the final compensation trial ￼ ￼. Georgia, South Carolina, and Alabama require a resolution or specific authorization by a governing body before condemnation, often with notice to the owner; Georgia additionally requires blight designations (when used) to be certified by official action. Notice periods in the South are typically well-defined and longer than in some other regions: e.g. Alabama and Mississippi require advance notice of intent and a waiting period, and Texas as noted enforces strict timelines for offers and notice by certified mail ￼ ￼. These measures all serve to ensure the owner is aware and has time to respond before litigation. Once in court, many southern states use commissioner panels or special masters to initially determine compensation (Texas uses court-appointed special commissioners to hear evidence and set a value), with the right for either side to appeal that award to a jury trial in court. The net effect is a highly structured initiation process, focused on transparency and giving owners time to understand their rights.
	•	Compensation Rules: The South is known for strong “make whole” compensation standards. Florida’s constitution uniquely guarantees “full compensation” for property taken (not merely “just” compensation) ￼. In practice, this means Florida compensates not only for the property’s fair market value and severance damages, but also obligates the government to pay owners’ reasonable costs and fees. Florida law requires the condemning authority to pay all attorney’s fees, expert fees, and court costs in eminent domain cases, on top of the compensation for the property ￼ ￼. Those fees in Florida are typically calculated on a sliding scale of the benefit achieved for the owner, and they do not reduce the owner’s award – they are paid separately by the condemnor ￼. Many Southern states have similar pro-owner fee provisions: e.g. Georgia and Mississippi require condemning agencies to cover owner’s court costs and fees if the final award exceeds the pre-suit offer by a certain margin. Texas defines “adequate compensation” as fair market value, including the value of the land taken plus damage to any remaining property (i.e. diminution in value of the remainder) ￼ ￼. While Texas does not automatically pay an owner’s attorney fees in every case, it does provide relocation payments for displaced owners (moving expenses, etc., pursuant to the federal Uniform Relocation Act) ￼. Notably, Virginia amended its constitution in 2012 to require that the owner be compensated for lost profits and lost access when land is taken (these are in addition to the real estate value) ￼ ￼. Virginia’s statutes set out a process for business owners to claim demonstrable lost profits for up to three years, and for property owners to claim loss of access to their remaining land, as part of just compensation. In Alabama, by contrast, the compensation is more bare-bones: just fair market value of the property and severance damages, with no reimbursement for attorney fees or business losses (Alabama is considered less protective – business losses are not recoverable and each side bears its own legal fees ￼ ￼). Many southern states also provide higher compensation for certain categories: Louisiana permits up to a 25% premium for homes taken under certain conditions (to offset subjective value to owners) ￼, and Missouri (often classed with the Midwest) similarly provides 125% of value for longtime residences ￼. In summary, the South overall strives for full indemnification of the owner: paying not just for the land, but for the collateral costs of losing the property (fees, moving costs, business damages, etc. where allowed). Property is typically valued at highest and best use, and severance damages (value loss to remaining land) are fully included ￼ ￼.
	•	Owner Rights & Notice: Southern states emphasize owner awareness and due process. For instance, Texas and Florida both publish a “Bill of Rights” for property owners facing condemnation, detailing their rights to notice, appraisal, negotiation, and jury trial ￼ ￼. Owners universally have the right to a jury trial on compensation in this region – this right is constitutional in some states (e.g. Georgia’s constitution guarantees jury determination of value). The timeframe for notice is generous: Texas requires that the Landowner’s Bill of Rights be provided at least 7 days before the final offer ￼, and the final offer itself must be made at least 30 days after the initial offer, giving owners time to obtain counsel and their own appraisal ￼. Florida’s statutes similarly require a notice of intent and an order of taking hearing in court; owners are entitled to contest the taking at that hearing (though if the project is for a valid public use and necessity, the challenge may be difficult). Many southern states give owners the right to challenge the public use or necessity in court without an initial presumption in favor of the government. For example, Virginia allows owners to contest whether the stated use is truly a public use, and the burden is on the condemnor to prove public use without any automatic presumption ￼ ￼. Owners can also argue that the taking is excessive – Virginia law lets an owner object if more property is taken than needed for the project ￼. In North Carolina (which only recently added an explicit eminent domain constitutional clause), owners must receive notice and have the right to be heard in a hearing before property is condemned, especially for blight or redevelopment takings which now require judicial approval. The appeal process for owners in the South is typically two-tiered: after the initial compensation award (by commissioners or at a hearing), an owner can appeal to the trial court and then up through the appellate courts. Throughout the process, southern states require that owners be given clear, written notices of their rights and the key steps – reinforcing procedural due process at every stage. The combination of up-front information, ample time to respond, and the ability to recover costs (in many cases) if they litigate, gives property owners in the South a strong position to protect their interests.
	•	Permissible Takings (Public Use): In the wake of Kelo v. New London, the South led the charge in restricting eminent domain to true public uses. Florida enacted both a statute and a constitutional amendment in 2006 that ban eminent domain for transferring property to a private party (i.e. for economic development) ￼. Florida even eliminated the use of blight as a justification – state law §73.014 flatly prohibits takings “to eliminate nuisance, slum, or blight conditions” if the takings are for private redevelopment ￼. Texas similarly amended its constitution (Art. I §17) and statutes to state that public use excludes takings for economic development or to enhance tax revenues ￼. Texas law now only permits eminent domain for clear public uses (e.g. infrastructure, utilities, public buildings) and forbids any taking “if the primary purpose is economic development,” with narrow exceptions for genuine blight clearance and certain common carriers ￼. Virtually every Southern state enacted comparable measures: Georgia’s constitution was amended to tighten the definition of public use and requires a higher approval process for any blight condemnations; South Carolina and Alabama amended their constitutions to disallow private-to-private property takings (except where necessary for public health/safety or actual blight). Virginia’s 2012 constitutional amendment is particularly strong: it defines public use narrowly (no taking “for private gain, private benefit, private enterprise, increasing jobs or tax revenue” is allowed) and even specifies that if land is taken for public service companies or utilities, the public’s right to use must be paramount ￼ ￼. It also codifies that blight elimination takings require the property itself to be blighted (no blight takings of non-blighted property just because it’s in a blighted area) ￼. Mississippi was slightly late to reform (it passed a constitutional amendment by voter initiative in 2011) but now prohibits transfer of taken property to private entities for 10 years after the taking, with limited exceptions (preventing subterfuge around the ban). In sum, Southern states overwhelmingly forbid eminent domain for economic development purposes – property cannot be condemned just to hand it to a developer or for general economic projects ￼. Public use must be literal (public ownership or use, or a recognized public necessity like roads or utility lines). A few exceptions remain, like truly blighted properties can be taken (e.g. a structurally unsafe building can be condemned and the land reused privately, but states often require rigorous blight definitions and processes for that). The southern region’s reforms are among the most protective of private property rights in the nation, ensuring that eminent domain is exercised only for traditional public uses or to remedy concrete threats to public welfare, while also expanding compensation and procedural rights for owners.

West
	•	Procedural Initiation: Western states typically follow formalized eminent domain procedures codified in statute, often similar to California’s Eminent Domain Law. There is an emphasis on demonstrating “necessity” and careful planning before a taking. For example, California requires that any public entity wishing to condemn property adopt a Resolution of Necessity after notice to the owner and a public hearing ￼. By law, the agency’s governing body must find: (1) the public project is necessary, (2) the project is planned to minimize private injury, and (3) the property to be taken is necessary for the project ￼ ￼. Only after such findings (usually requiring a supermajority vote) can the entity file a court action. Other Western states (e.g. Nevada, Arizona, Colorado) similarly require either a public vote or specific findings of public necessity and use before eminent domain is invoked. Pre-condemnation offers and appraisals are universally required in the West: California law forces the condemning agency to provide the owner with a written appraisal summary and make an offer not less than that appraised value, and to negotiate in good faith before suing ￼ ￼. If the owner rejects the offer, many western states allow a “quick take” procedure – for instance, California and Arizona permit the agency to deposit the probable just compensation with the court and obtain early possession of the property, after which the owner may withdraw the deposit while the final value is litigated ￼ ￼. Notice to the owner is a critical component: statutes often mandate that owners receive copies of appraisals and detailed descriptions of the project. In Nevada, any entity must first attempt to negotiate and then provide a notice of intent to seek an order of possession. Colorado and Utah require a formal notice period (often 30 days) after final offer before filing condemnation. These states also commonly require mediation or alternate dispute resolution to be offered before trial. Overall, Western states’ initiation procedures are characterized by structured resolutions or findings, transparent offers backed by appraisal, and often a court hearing to confirm public use/necessity (especially if contested by the owner) prior to issuing an order of condemnation.
	•	Compensation Rules: Just compensation in Western states is determined under similar principles (fair market value, highest and best use, plus severance damages for partial takings). However, some Western states have noteworthy additional compensation elements. California stands out for allowing compensation for loss of business goodwill in a taking. Under California law, if a business is conducted on the taken property, the owner of the business can recover the value of goodwill lost due to the condemnation (provided the loss cannot reasonably be avoided by relocating the business) ￼ ￼. This is a statutory right in California and is in addition to the property’s market value. Many Western states (e.g. Nevada, Arizona, Oregon) have aligned with the federal Uniform Relocation Act, providing payments for relocation costs, re-establishment expenses for businesses, and replacement housing differentials for displaced homeowners. Nevada’s constitution, after reform, uniquely requires that fair market value be determined for the “highest and best use” of the property, not just current use, ensuring owners receive full value if their property had potential for more valuable use (this was part of the 2008 “PISTOL” amendment aimed at preventing undervaluation). Nevada also mandates compensation for any reduction in value to a remaining parcel and, in certain cases, requires exceptional compensation – for example, some interpretations of the Nevada reforms suggest owners of homes taken may be entitled to more than 100% of value (to be “made whole”), though the text mainly solidified highest-use valuation ￼. In Washington, while business losses per se aren’t compensated, the law does ensure recovery for personal property relocation and improvements. Alaska and Hawaii generally follow standard just compensation rules (market value and damages); Hawaii notably has used eminent domain for public programs (e.g. land redistribution) but compensates at market value as determined by negotiation or condemnation trial. Attorney fee recovery in the West is a mixed picture: California will award litigation expenses (including attorney and expert fees) to the owner if the condemnor’s final offer was unreasonable and the owner’s demand was reasonable in light of the trial result ￼ ￼. This “reasonableness” test (California Code of Civil Procedure §1250.410) is meant to encourage fair offers; if the government low-balled, the owner gets fees. Oregon allows recovery of attorney fees if the final judgment exceeds the condemner’s highest written offer by a statutory threshold. Arizona and New Mexico likewise provide for owner’s fees and costs when owners prevail in challenging a taking or when the award is significantly above the offer. In contrast, Utah and Idaho generally do not grant fees except in cases of bad faith or if a condemnation is abandoned. As for valuation techniques, Western states are aligned in using fair market value, considering all reasonably probable uses. Severance damages (for the devaluation of the remainder) are fully recognized, and benefits to the remainder can sometimes offset those damages (except states like California and Idaho disallow speculative “general” benefits from projects as offsets – only specific measurable benefits to the remaining property can offset damages).
	•	Owner Rights & Notice: Owners in the West enjoy robust rights to contest and be heard, though these rights must be exercised within the legal process. Many western states allow an explicit hearing or motion on the right to take. For example, Arizona permits owners to request a court hearing to challenge the legality of the taking (public use or necessity) before proceeding to valuation; Nevada’s constitutional amendment requires “public use” to be proven by the government with clear and convincing evidence in court if an owner challenges it. Washington State requires that any eminent domain for economic redevelopment be approved by a supermajority of the government body, effectively giving owners a political avenue to contest. Notice to property owners is highly formalized: California’s law demands personal service or certified mail of the Resolution of Necessity and a 20-day notice before the hearing on that resolution, allowing the owner to appear and voice objections ￼ ￼. In Colorado, an owner must be given a written notice at least 30 days before any court action, and notice of the compensation commission’s hearing. Western states also provide for jury trials on compensation if the owner desires – except a few like California where just compensation is determined in a jury trial by default (and the jury’s verdict is final on value). In Oregon and Washington, owners can elect jury determination as well. If dissatisfied with a trial result, owners can appeal to higher courts (though typically on legal issues, since compensation amounts set by jury are rarely overturned if procedurally sound). Importantly, owners’ rights to relocation assistance are strongly protected in the West: under federal and often parallel state law, displaced owners and tenants must receive advance notice before being required to move, and cannot be forced out until adequate replacement housing or relocation payment is provided. Hawaii and Alaska follow these federal relocation rules for any projects involving federal funds (which covers most public projects). In summary, Western property owners are well-informed (by formal notices and disclosure of appraisals), have opportunities to object to a taking (administratively and judicially), and can insist on a jury for valuation. However, once a project is deemed truly necessary and for public use, courts in the West, as elsewhere, will not second-guess legislative decisions – the focus then shifts to ensuring the owner gets fair compensation. The Western ethos of open government and public participation is reflected in the eminent domain process, giving owners a voice at public hearings and in court, and even the ability to recover fees if the government’s positions were unjustified.
	•	Permissible Takings (Public Use): Western states, many through voter initiatives, imposed firm limits on takings for private benefit. Nevada’s constitution (Article 1, §22, added in 2006/2008) is one of the strictest: it declares that public use does not include taking unblighted property for private economic development, and it requires that any eminent domain predominantly benefiting a private party be invalid. It even entitles property owners to repurchase their property at the condemnation price if the public use is not achieved in 5 years. Arizona voters approved Proposition 207 (2006) which, among other things, forbids eminent domain for private use unless it is to eliminate a direct threat to public health or safety (i.e. truly blighted conditions). Oregon’s Measure 39 (2006) similarly prohibits public bodies from condemning private property for transfer to another private party, with only narrow exceptions (such as utility projects) ￼. These laws mean pure economic development takings are outlawed in much of the West. Washington did not amend its constitution, but court decisions and statutes now prevent takings for economic development except in blighted areas (and Washington narrowed the definition of “blight” to prevent abuse). California, notably, took a more moderate approach: in 2008, voters passed Proposition 99, a constitutional amendment that forbids the use of eminent domain to acquire an owner-occupied single-family residence for transfer to a private person ￼. This was a limited reform – it protects homes, but not other property types, from being taken for private development. California had already dissolved redevelopment agencies in 2012 (partly for fiscal reasons), which reduced but did not eliminate the use of eminent domain for redevelopment projects. Thus, in California and a few others, economic development takings are curtailed but not as comprehensively banned as in the rest of the West. Utah, Idaho, Montana, Wyoming all passed statutes post-Kelo limiting eminent domain to defined public uses and barring takings for private enterprise (with allowances for utilities and common carriers). For example, Utah Code §78B-6-501 was amended to exclude using eminent domain for trails or paths to increase property values, and generally Utah disallows municipal takings for retail/commercial development. Colorado’s reform requires blight to be proven on a property-by-property basis by clear and convincing evidence before a redevelopment condemnation – no more declaring whole neighborhoods blighted unless each parcel meets criteria. Across the West, public use is interpreted more narrowly now: taking property simply to confer it on another private party for economic gain is broadly seen as unconstitutional or unlawful ￼. Traditional public uses (infrastructure, public buildings, schools, parks, utilities) remain fully allowable. One nuanced area is energy infrastructure – several western states grapple with pipeline and transmission line projects (often built by private companies). Those are generally permitted as public uses (because they serve the public at large), but states like Nevada and Idaho have reinforced landowner protections in those contexts (e.g. strict necessity tests and generous compensation for any easements taken). In conclusion, the Western region joins the national trend: post-Kelo, 45 states (including virtually all in the West) have reformed eminent domain laws to prevent abuse and protect property owners ￼. The West achieved this largely via constitutional amendments and citizen initiatives, reflecting a strong public sentiment for safeguarding private property against unjust takings.

California	Public entity must adopt a Resolution of Necessity after a noticed public hearing (owner gets 15–20 days’ notice) ￼. The resolution requires 3 findings: public use, least private injury, necessity of the property ￼. An offer based on an appraisal must be made  at least 20 days before the hearing ￼. After the resolution, the entity files a court action. Quick-take allowed: after lawsuit, deposit of probable compensation lets government take early possession ￼.	Fair market value (“highest price” in an open market, considering highest and best use) ￼. Severance damages for remaining property are paid ￼. California uniquely compensates loss of business goodwill for businesses on the property ￼ (owner must prove loss and inability to mitigate). Litigation expenses (owner’s attorney, expert fees) are awarded if owner’s final demand was reasonable and government’s offer was unreasonable relative to trial result ￼ ￼. Relocation assistance is provided per California Relocation Assistance Act (parallel to federal).	Owner can object to the Resolution of Necessity at the public hearing and can challenge the taking in court (by writ of mandate) within 30 days of resolution, though success is rare. Full jury trial on compensation as a matter of right ￼ ￼. Owner not liable for government’s costs ￼. Notice: personal service or certified mail of hearing notice, appraisal summary provided. If quick-take used, owner gets notice of deposit and can withdraw funds immediately.	Public use defined broadly (“use concerning the whole community”); California did limited reform: Prop 99 (2008) bars taking an owner-occupied single-family home for conveyance to a private party ￼. Otherwise, economic development takings are not outright banned if a public purpose is asserted (e.g. redevelopment), but redevelopment requires area be blighted. (CA dissolved its redevelopment agencies in 2012, curbing widespread private transfer takings.) Utilities and railroads can condemn for public service.
Colorado	A governing body (city/county) must pass an ordinance or resolution authorizing eminent domain, with notice to affected owners. An appraisal and good-faith offer are required before filing. If contested, a court decides public use and necessity. Commissioners (three appraisers) initially determine compensation; either party may request a jury trial afterward.	Fair market value at highest and best use. Partial takings include severance damages minus any special benefits. No statutory bonus on value. Attorney fees awarded to owner if final award ≥ 130% of last offer (CRS 38-1-122). Relocation payments provided under state law for displaced owners/tenants (comparable to federal URA).	Owners can file a motion to dismiss for lack of public use/necessity; CO law (post-2006) puts burden on condemnor to prove blight by clear and convincing evidence for redevelopment takings. Jury trial available on value. Owners get notice of the intended ordinance and 30 days’ notice before the possession date if quick-take.	2006 reforms: no condemnation for economic development unless property is in a blighted area (and blight must be proven parcel by parcel). Blight definition tightened – can’t declare an area blighted just due to economic underperformance. Transfers to private developers are largely prohibited absent true blight.
Connecticut	Before Kelo, CT required a public hearing and finding of public necessity for redevelopment takings. After Kelo, reforms require that any development taking must have a predominant public purpose and cannot be solely for increasing tax revenue. Condemnation is initiated by a development agency with legislative approval. In other cases (roads, etc.), the state or town must authorize and file in court.	Fair market value is paid. CT now mandates additional moving expense payments and allows certain displaced homeowners an added payment to assist in purchasing a replacement dwelling (to ensure they are made whole). No multiplier on FMV in law. Attorney fees are not generally awarded except in inverse condemnation or if statute specifically allows for abandoned takings.	Owners are entitled to a notice of taking by recorded lis pendens and certified mail. They have 30 days to appeal a statement of compensation (which is filed by the agency in a quick-take style proceeding). A judge (or referee) hears appeals on compensation; no jury provided in CT (valuation by court). The owner can contest public use via administrative appeal of the legislative finding, but under the new law, economic development alone is not sufficient public use. Notice and Relocation benefits are extensive under CT Gen Stat. §8-268 et seq.	Public use must be primary. Post-Kelo law (2007) prohibits takings where the primary purpose is economic development. Any private benefit must be incidental. Blight takings: still allowed, but CT narrowed the definition of blight and requires specific findings for each property. Overall, CT now leans against private-to-private takings; projects must address a public problem (e.g. removing real blight) or be for public ownership.
Delaware	The condemning agency (often the Delaware DOT or local government) must appraise and make a good faith offer. If no agreement, a condemnation suit is filed in Superior Court. No separate commission — valuation is by jury trial by default in DE. Quick-take is available for state agencies (with court approval) to take title upon deposit.	Fair market value (willing buyer/seller). DE does not provide enhanced value multipliers. It includes severance damages for any decrease in value of the remainder. Business losses are not compensated (except trade fixtures at market value). Attorney fees: Delaware generally does not award them, except if a condemnation is found unauthorized. Relocation assistance as per federal guidelines for those displaced.	Owners get a written offer and at least 60 days to consider (DOT policy). After filing, they receive summons and can contest public use in court. Delaware’s courts will scrutinize if the use is truly public, especially after recent reforms. Jury trial is guaranteed on compensation. Notice of intent to condemn is often given via a formal letter well prior to suit (especially for DOT projects).	One of first to reform post-Kelo (2005): Delaware law now forbids eminent domain “for private economic development.” Takings are only for public use – defined to exclude increasing employment, tax revenue or economic health ￼. Essentially no takings to transfer land to private developers. The only exception is removal of blighted property that poses a danger.
Florida	A good faith presuit negotiation is required by Fla. Stat. §73.015 – the condemnor must attempt to reach an agreement and cannot file unless the owner rejects a written offer ￼ ￼. If suit filed, a quick-take procedure (Chapter 74) is typically used: the agency seeks an Order of Taking at a hearing where it must show public purpose, reasonable necessity, and a valid appraisal-based estimate ￼. Upon entry of the order and deposit of the estimate, title transfers to the condemnor ￼. The owner can then withdraw the deposit and later have a jury trial to determine full compensation.	“Full compensation” required by Florida’s Constitution (Art. X §6(a)), interpreted to include all elements necessary to make the owner whole ￼ ￼. This includes fair market value of property taken, severance damages to remainder ￼, plus reasonable attorneys’ fees, appraisal fees, and costs paid by condemnor ￼. It also explicitly covers moving costs and other incidental damages ￼ ￼. Fair market value in FL considers highest and best use and even “stigma” damages (e.g. fear or perception affecting value) in severance ￼ ￼. Business damages (lost business value) are payable only in partial takings of certain businesses (per statute §73.071(3)(b) for businesses of 5+ years on the property).	Owners have the right to be heard at the Order of Taking hearing (to contest public purpose or necessity) ￼, although overturning a taking is rare if statutory steps are met. They have an absolute right to a jury trial on compensation ￼. Florida provides extensive notice: a condemnor must give at least 30 days’ notice of the Order of Taking hearing and disclose appraisal reports prior ￼. The property owner also receives a written Landowner Bill of Rights detailing their rights. If the owner disagrees with the compensation after the deposit, they can litigate without risking payment of their fees (since the condemning authority pays them). Appeal: owners can appeal a taking on legal grounds and are entitled to appellate attorney fees if they prevail ￼.	Florida has one of the strongest prohibitions on private takings. 2006 reforms (HB 1567 and a constitutional amendment) prohibit eminent domain for transferring property to a private party. Public use in Florida must be literal – use by the public or government. Even blight elimination takings were outlawed: Fla. Stat. §73.014 “prohibits the use of eminent domain to eliminate nuisance, slum, or blight conditions” as a standalone justification ￼. Thus, no taking solely for economic redevelopment is allowed ￼. (The only slight exception: private utility companies retain eminent domain for corridors, as quasi-public entities.)
Georgia	After 2006 reforms, any use of eminent domain for redevelopment requires approval by a local elected governing body and a finding of blight for each specific property. Generally, an agency must provide the owner with an appraisal and written offer. If no agreement, the agency files a petition in superior court. A court hearing is held (especially if public use is contested or if a quick-take is sought). Quick-take (“declaration of taking”) is available for certain projects: the agency files a declaration and deposit; title vests immediately, subject to later compensation determination.	Fair market value at highest and best use. Georgia allows compensation for fixtures or equipment that cannot be relocated (treated as part of real estate value). Severance damages are included for any decrease in value of remaining land. Business losses are not compensable (unless a fixture is taken). Attorney fees are generally not awarded unless the taking is found improper (then owner can get fees). Relocation assistance is provided in accordance with federal Uniform Act for projects receiving federal funds (state law adopts similar provisions).	Owners have a constitutional right to a jury determination of compensation (Ga. Const. Art. I, § III). Georgia law also gives owners the right to challenge the public use in court; post-2006, if an owner claims the taking is for economic development, the condemnor must prove a valid public use (blight or traditional use) – there’s no automatic presumption. Notice: for redevelopment takings, each property owner must get notice of the blight designation and upcoming vote. In standard takings, a written notice of intent and a 30-day negotiation period are required before filing. If quick-take used, owner gets notice of declaration and can immediately appeal for more compensation while funds are available to withdraw.	Georgia’s 2006 constitutional amendment and statute bar economic development takings. Public use excludes takings for private development or enterprise. Eminent domain for redevelopment now requires that each parcel be certified as blighted (meeting a strict definition) by an independent finder (housing authority or similar), and then approved by a city/county governing body ￼. If a property is not itself blighted, it cannot be taken for a private redevelopment project. Traditional public uses (roads, schools, utilities) are unaffected and proceed normally.
Hawaii	Eminent domain in HI is often exercised by the state or counties via an enabling ordinance or statute (e.g., Hawaii Housing Authority had special condemnation powers). The government must present a public purpose and attempt negotiation. If proceeding, it files in circuit court. Historically, Hawaii utilized a “quick-take” (called “advance possession”): upon filing and depositing estimated compensation, the government can get immediate possession with court approval.	Fair market value standard. Hawaii gained notoriety with Midkiff (1984) for using eminent domain to break up land oligopolies – in those cases, compensation was market value paid by new homeowners. No special enhancements; the value is as of date of summons. Severance damages apply if only part taken. No statutory compensation for goodwill or business losses. Attorney fees are generally not awarded (except if the taking was not authorized or in certain inverse condemnation cases by statute). Relocation assistance follows federal guidelines for any federally funded projects; state law also provides relocation benefits for certain housing condemnations.	Owners can contest the public use in court, but Hawaii courts apply a very deferential standard (any conceivable public purpose can satisfy, per Midkiff). Jury trials are available on compensation (either party can request). Notice: Hawaii requires that a condemnation ordinance be published and affected owners notified (often by mail and newspaper) before adoption. Once in court, owners get standard civil process notice. In land reform cases, notice was given to all lessees/lessors involved. Owners do have a right to appear and be heard at any legislative hearing authorizing a project.	Hawaii uses eminent domain for broad public purposes (including reducing concentrated land ownership, as upheld in Haw. Hous. Auth. v. Midkiff). There have been no recent legislative changes post-Kelo, likely because HI already primarily uses condemnation for public housing, infrastructure, or unique public goals. While not explicitly forbidding economic development takings, in practice Hawaii’s use is limited to clear public programs (and private-transfer takings like pure commercial redevelopment are not common in Hawaii). So, de facto public use is interpreted broadly, but political controls limit purely private takings.
Idaho	Idaho requires that any entity with eminent domain authority (state or local) first negotiate with the owner. Statute forbids filing a condemnation complaint without an attempt to purchase in good faith. If filed, the court will hold a hearing on public use if the owner objects. Idaho often appoints three commissioners to determine compensation, with right to jury trial appeal.	Fair market value at highest and best use. Idaho law specifically excludes enhancement of value to remaining property by the project from being offset against severance damages (so owners get full severance damages). No compensation for loss of goodwill or business profits (Idaho courts deem those too speculative, unless they affect real property value). Attorney fees can be awarded to the owner if the final award exceeds the condemnor’s highest offer by a certain margin (or if the condemnation was not sufficiently authorized). Relocation assistance provided per federal standards for projects with federal money; state law (I.C. §7-701 et seq.) provides some relocation payments for others.	Owners may challenge the necessity of the taking; Idaho law (I.C. §7-704) requires the condemnor to show the taking is necessary and for a public use. Owners get notice of condemnation via service of process; no separate requirement for advanced public hearing (unless it’s a city taking requiring a resolution). Jury trial available on appeal of commissioners’ award. Notice and a reasonable time to respond to offers are mandated. If project is abandoned or dismissed, owner can motion for reimbursement of costs.	Idaho enacted a ban on eminent domain for private economic development in 2006. Idaho Code §7-701A prohibits using eminent domain “to acquire private property for any alleged public use that is merely a pretext…for the transfer of the property to a private party.” Essentially, economic development, increased tax base, or job creation by themselves are not valid public uses. Legitimate public uses (roads, public facilities, utilities) and removing dangerous structures are allowed.
Illinois	Illinois mandates that for most takings (except state DOT), the condemning authority must attempt negotiation and then file a complaint in circuit court. After 2006 reforms, if the taking is for blight/redevelopment, it requires prior blight designation and public hearings. Illinois uses a quick-take procedure for certain projects (particularly transportation) – via a motion, the court vests title in the condemnor upon deposit of preliminary compensation (often within  quick statutory timelines for highways). Otherwise, a trial on compensation is held.	Fair market value, considering highest and best use. Illinois law (post-reform) requires clear and convincing evidence of blight/value for redevelopment takings, which indirectly ensures higher valuations for urban renewal. Severance damages are allowed. If the condemnor does not ultimately use the property, Illinois must offer to sell it back at the price paid. Attorney fees: the Eminent Domain Act (735 ILCS 30) lets owners recover fees if the final compensation exceeds the condemnor’s offer by at least 25%. Relocation assistance provided (Illinois adopted the federal URA provisions for state projects).	Owners can request a quick judicial review of public use (especially if it’s a redevelopment taking—courts will verify blight findings). Jury trial is guaranteed on compensation. Notice: for redevelopment, extensive notice and public hearing process before approval. In standard cases, notice of intent to file is often given, but not statutorily required beyond the offer. Once filed, the owner is served and a trial date is set. Illinois also allows interlocutory appeals on the issue of public use/necessity in some cases.	Illinois law now states economic development by itself is not public use. A 2006 Act tightened definitions: “public use” expressly excludes taking for private ownership or control, unless to eliminate blighted areas (and blight must be proven parcel by parcel). Even for blight, there are additional procedural hurdles and compensation incentives to dissuade misuse ￼. Thus, no takings purely to confer a private benefit or increase tax revenue. Traditional uses and true blight clearance remain valid.
Indiana	Indiana requires condemning authorities to issue a formal Notice of Intent to the owner and, for municipal takings, obtain approval from the city/town council or county commissioners. An appraisal and offer are required before suit. If the owner does not accept within 30 days, a complaint is filed. Court appoints appraisers to assess damages; either party can then demand a jury trial on those damages. For certain highway projects, a quick-take by deposit is allowed.	Fair market value based on the property’s appropriate use. Indiana permits recovery for loss of ingress/egress in partial takings (treated as damage to remainder). Business losses are not separately compensated. By statute, if the final award is more than the appraisers’ award by a substantial amount, owner can get litigation expenses (Indiana Code §32-24-1-14). Relocation costs are available as per federal guidelines, and Indiana has state laws for relocation assistance payments.	Owners have the right to object to the taking via a court proceeding (within the eminent domain case). Indiana’s post-Kelo law requires court findings if a taking is for a “private commercial facility”; owners can argue the taking is unlawful if primarily for private use. Notice: the initial Notice of Intent must be sent by certified mail at least 30 days before filing, giving owners time to negotiate or object at the local approval stage. After filing, owners are served and can file objections within 30 days. Jury trial available on appeal of appraisers’ award.	Indiana’s 2006 reform prohibits using eminent domain for purely private commercial uses. A property cannot be condemned for transfer to another private entity unless it is in an area certified as blighted (and Indiana narrowed the definition of blight). Even then, each taking for a private entity (like for a shopping center) must be specifically approved by the local legislative body with findings of public benefit. Increasing tax base or jobs alone is not sufficient public use.
Iowa	Iowa’s law (modified in 2006) requires an authority to give notice to owners and hold a public hearing for certain takings (especially if for economic development). A 90-day notice of intent is required for agricultural land. After notice and offer, condemnation is initiated by an application to the chief judge, who appoints a compensation commission (commissioners) to assess damages. The commission’s award can then be appealed to district court for a jury trial.	Fair market value at highest and best use. Iowa permits consideration of agricultural productivity for farm land value. Severance damages and loss of access are compensable. If a family farm homestead is taken, Iowa law grants a preference to acquire an easement to access remaining land or potentially an offset for the cost of acquiring equivalent land. No explicit additional multipliers. Attorney fees: if the jury award is >=110% of the commission award, owner can recover fees up to a cap (I.C. §6B.33).	Owners can file objections to the condemnation with the district court, challenging public use or procedures. Notably, Iowa’s Supreme Court in County of Carroll v. Hoover (2006) established stricter scrutiny of takings for private development, giving owners a better chance to block such takings. Owners receive extensive notice: written notice of public hearing by mail and publication (for certain projects) and at least 30 days’ notice before the compensation commission hearing. Jury trial is available on appeal of the award.	After Kelo, Iowa enacted a law preventing eminent domain for economic development unless the property is blighted (and with a high bar for blight). Even for blight, if it’s agricultural land, additional restrictions apply (must be an urban slum/blight, not just productive farm). Iowa courts have also been protective: they will strike down takings where public use is a pretext for private gain ￼. Overall, no condemnations solely for private economic benefit.
Kansas	Since 2007, any exercise of eminent domain for private redevelopment in Kansas requires explicit legislative approval. Otherwise, for public projects, the procedure: the entity adopts a resolution, gives notice to owner, and files a petition in court. A appraiser panel determines initial compensation; parties can appeal to district court and demand jury trial. Quick-take is not generally available except for certain highway easements (with court order).	Fair market value standard. Kansas includes value of all interests taken (including leases, etc.). Partial takings: pays difference in value before vs. after. No statutory bonus on value. Attorney fees: if the jury award exceeds the appraisers’ award by >certain % (usually 125%), the owner may recover litigation costs (K.S.A. 26-509). Relocation benefits are provided under state law aligning with federal Uniform Act.	Owners get notice of the initial condemnation petition and can file written defenses objecting to the taking. The court will hear objections – Kansas law gives owners standing to argue lack of public use or necessity. Since any private-use takings must go through the state legislature, owners effectively have political protection in those cases. Notice: at least 30 days before filing, a final offer is typically required. Also, public announcement of the project is common via city meetings. Jury trial available on appeal of value.	Kansas passed a constitutional amendment (Art. 15 §18) and statute in 2006: eminent domain cannot be used to transfer property to a private entity without a specific act of the legislature. In practice, that has halted local governments from doing economic development takings. The legislature has to review and approve each such project, which so far has been a high hurdle. Traditional public uses proceed normally. Blighted property takings for private reuse now require legislative approval too, effectively banning them unless the state intervenes.
Kentucky	Kentucky law requires a condemnor to attempt negotiation and then file a petition in circuit court. Certain agencies (like redevelopment authorities) must obtain local government approval and certify blight if applicable. The court appoints commissioners to award initial compensation; either party may appeal to circuit court for a jury trial. No quick-take except for highways (where the state can take possession  after 20 days from petition if bond posted).	Fair market value is paid. Kentucky includes incidental damages and benefits set-off (special benefits to remainder can offset severance damages). No extra percentage compensation in law. If the award on appeal is > 125% of the commissioners’ award, the owner can recover court costs and attorney fees (KRS 416.260). Business losses are not separately compensated (except possibly as part of fixture value). Relocation assistance follows federal requirements for major projects.	Owners can contest the right to take; KY courts require public use and public necessity – in 2006, the legislature added that condemnations for private use are not allowed, aligning with a stricter public-use test. Owners get notice of the petition and a chance to file exceptions to the commissioners’ report (which can include challenging the taking itself). Jury trial for value if demanded. Notice and hearing are required for redevelopment takings (blight designation has a hearing process).	Kentucky’s General Assembly in 2006 tightened eminent domain: it prohibited takings for private development or to increase tax revenue. Public use must be strictly interpreted – KY law now lists allowable public uses and states economic development is not one of them ￼. Blight takings are allowed but with narrower criteria (must be a danger to health/safety, not just old or underused property). So, no condemnation primarily for private commercial enterprise.
Louisiana	Louisiana requires a public body to determine and declare a public purpose via ordinance or resolution. Post-Kelo amendments demand a hearing and public notice for any taking that would eventually vest in a private entity (like industrial development – which is mostly forbidden now). Condemnation suits are filed in district court. Louisiana has a quick-take mechanism, especially for transportation projects: the DOTD can file an ex parte order of taking and deposit the estimate to take title immediately (under RS 48:441 et seq.).	Louisiana’s Constitution (Art. I §4) was amended in 2006 to strengthen compensation: it requires “just compensation” to be at least the fair market value and adds that for an owner’s primary residence taken for public purposes, the owner is entitled to an additional 25% of that value as a “residential location factor” (to mitigate inconvenience) ￼. Thus, certain homeowners get 125% of FMV. Severance damages are fully payable. Lost business profits and other losses are generally not compensated (unless covered as part of severance or under specific statutes for some business takings). Attorney fees are not automatically paid, but if a taking is found invalid, the owner can recover fees. Relocation assistance is provided by state law for those displaced (La. RS 9:3391).	Owners have the right to challenge the taking in court; Louisiana’s constitution now explicitly allows challenges on grounds of lack of public use or if more property is taken than needed. Jury trial is available on compensation (except in quick-take where initial compensation is set by court-appointed experts, but owner can then appeal for jury). Notice: constitution requires that owners get reasonable notice and an opportunity for a hearing before the taking of their home for public purposes (to contest public purpose/blight). For standard takings, notice of intent and offer must be given, and in quick-take, notice of deposit is served to owner.	Louisiana’s 2006 constitutional amendment and laws make it clear: no property shall be taken for predominant use by any private person or entity. Public use excludes taking for economic development, enhancement of tax revenue or job creation. The amendment lists allowable public uses (public facilities, roads, utilities, elimination of a threat to public health/safety, removal of public nuisances or structures beyond repair). Even blight takings were restricted: property can only be taken to remove a threat to public health or safety caused by the property. And if taken for such reason, it cannot be transferred to a private party unless first offered to the original owner to buy back. In short, Louisiana essentially banned Kelo-type takings.
Maine	Eminent domain in Maine is used sparingly. A condemning authority (state agency or municipality) must provide notice to the owner and the public, and typically hold a hearing or public meeting to declare the public use. After that, the agency makes an offer and, if not accepted, files a condemnation notice and plat. Maine often uses an administrative taking process: the condemnor records an order of condemnation and deposits damages; the owner can then appeal the damage award to court.	Fair market value at highest use. Maine pays for the property taken and damages to remaining property. No statutory additional percentage on value. Maine law does allow recovery of reasonable appraisal costs by the owner in certain cases. Attorney fees are generally not awarded except if the taking was invalid or if a statutory offer process (for state highway takings) triggers fee-shifting (e.g., if the final award is 30% higher than the state’s offer, the owner may get fees). Relocation assistance as per federal URA for federally aided projects; state has similar provisions for state-only projects.	Owners may file an appeal in the Superior Court to challenge either the right to take or the adequacy of damages. Maine’s Supreme Judicial Court in Bell v. Town of Wells (2006) held that takings primarily to confer a private benefit violate the Maine Constitution, so owners have that precedent to invoke if needed. Notice must be given to owners prior to the taking (typically 30 days notice of a hearing or of the proposed condemnation order). If an administrative taking occurs (common for highways), the owner gets notice of the filed order and can appeal within the statutory period (usually 60–90 days for damages, shorter for public use challenge).	Maine did not need major legislative change post-Kelo because its courts already took a stance against purely private takings. By case law and practice, economic development alone is not a valid public use in Maine ￼. Takings must be for a recognized public purpose (roads, public facilities, etc.) or to eliminate a public harm. There was a 2006 constitutional resolution considered to explicitly ban private transferee takings, but even without it, in practice Maine authorities do not pursue Kelo-type condemnations. Blight/urban renewal takings are allowed but rarely used; if used, they would require genuine public necessity (and likely public ownership or use, given the legal climate).
Maryland	Maryland condemnation typically requires the government (state or local) to issue a formal resolution or ordinance authorizing the taking. An appraisal and offer to the owner are required by state law. If not settled, a petition is filed in circuit court. Maryland uses a “straight condemnation” (no commissioners; it goes directly to trial) unless quick-take is invoked. The City of Baltimore and State Highway Administration have quick-take authority in certain cases, allowing them to take title upon deposit of estimate and court order.	Fair market value (MD uses willing buyer/seller test). Includes value of improvements, and for partial takings, damages to remainder (and offsets for special benefits). No multiplier on value. Maryland does not provide for owner’s attorney fees in regular takings (each pays own costs, except if condemnor abandons the case, then it may owe limited costs). Business interruption is generally not compensated (aside from any effect on real property value). Relocation assistance is available under Maryland Relocation Act for displaced persons.	Owners have the right to trial by jury on valuation if they elect. Maryland recently implemented a “fast-track” appeal to contest a condemnation’s legality, but traditionally courts have been deferential on public use. Notice: a written offer must be open for 30 days before filing. Owners get served with the petition and notice of the right to contest. If quick-take is used (e.g. Baltimore for redevelopment), the owner gets notice of the immediate possession hearing, often only 7–10 days notice due to urgency. The owner can challenge necessity at that hearing.	Maryland did not pass sweeping changes post-Kelo. Efforts at reform stalled, though there were some tweaks (e.g. a 2007 law tightening the definition of “blighted area” for Baltimore’s uses). Generally, Maryland permits takings for redevelopment, but political pressure has curtailed abuse. Public use in MD includes traditional uses and urban renewal (with public hearings and blight findings). However, thanks to public sentiment, major economic-development takings are now approached cautiously. While not statutorily banned, a de facto policy shift makes pure private-benefit takings uncommon. (Thus, MD is among the few states that made only cosmetic or no statutory changes ￼; it relies on case-by-case scrutiny and local political processes to protect owners.)
Massachusetts	Massachusetts allows eminent domain via an order of taking by the responsible public authority (under M.G.L. c.79). No court action is needed to transfer title – the government records an order of taking, and title passes to the government, which must then notify the owner and offer damages. The owner can accept the proffered damages or sue for more in the Superior Court. Before an order is adopted, the agency typically provides notice and a public hearing (especially for redevelopment takings requiring a finding of “decadent” or blighted area).	Fair market value as of the date of taking (recording of the order). MA permits a broad view of damages: including value of property taken and “pro tanto” payments for appraised value upfront. If only part taken, damages = value of part + severance damages to the remainder. No statutory bonus or multipliers. Interest at a statutory rate accrues on additional damages awarded in court (which can be significant over time). Legal fees are not awarded in MA eminent domain cases (each party pays its own, even if owner wins more in court). Relocation benefits are provided under state law for displaced owners/tenants (M.G.L. c.79A).	Owners’ rights: they can challenge the taking’s validity via an equity action if they believe it’s not for public use (rarely successful, as MA courts defer if procedures are followed). Primarily, owners’ remedy is to seek higher compensation: they have 3 years to file a petition for assessment of damages if unsatisfied with the offer. There is no jury trial in MA; damages cases are bench trials (though either side can request that a commission of three disinterested referees hear the case instead). Notice: after an order of taking is recorded, the government must notify the owner and publish a notice; prior to the order, certain takings (like urban renewal plans) require public hearing and notice to affected owners.	Massachusetts did not enact new restrictions post-Kelo. It already required that takings for redevelopment occur only in “decadent, substandard or blighted” areas as officially found by municipal authorities. This remains the key limit – you cannot condemn property just to give to a private developer unless the area meets the blight criteria under Chapter 121B. Economic development alone is not stated as public use, but urban renewal projects that arguably have economic motives are allowed if blight is present. Thus, MA continues to allow takings for public purposes broadly, with procedural safeguards; it stands as one of the few states without a specific statutory prohibition on Kelo-type takings (beyond existing blight requirement).
Michigan	A condemning agency must have specific statutory authorization and must make a formal offer to the owner. If the owner doesn’t accept, the agency files a complaint in circuit court. Michigan uses a two-phase process: (1) “necessity” phase – the court (or sometimes a condemnation commission) determines if the taking is necessary and for public use, and (2) “valuation” phase – compensation determined by a jury (unless waived) ￼ ￼. Quick-take is allowed: the government may make a motion for “order vesting title” after necessity is determined, by depositing the amount of good-faith offer.	Enhanced compensation protections: Michigan’s Constitution (Art. X §2) requires payment of 125% of fair market value for owner-occupied principal residences taken ￼. It also guarantees reimbursement for some personal property moving expenses. Business losses: Michigan law compensates businesses for actual business interruption costs during relocation and, if a business cannot relocate, for loss of going concern value ￼. Fair market value itself is determined at the suit filing date ￼, and includes the property’s highest and best use. Severance damages are paid for partial takings ￼. Attorney fees and expert costs are reimbursed to the owner by statute ￼ – the condemnor must pay the owner’s reasonable fees, which encourages owners to seek full value without cost to them.	Owners can object to the necessity of the taking (and since 2006, the burden is on the agency to prove public use by preponderance – or by clear and convincing if it’s to remedy blight) ￼. Owners are entitled to a jury for determining just compensation ￼. Notice: owners must be given a good-faith written offer and a copy of the appraisal at least 45 days before filing. After filing, they get summons and have 21 days to contest necessity. Michigan also set up a quick timeline for condemnation cases (they are given priority on dockets). Importantly, if the agency abandons the taking or is found not to have authority, it must pay the owner’s expenses.	Michigan reacted decisively to Kelo. A 2006 constitutional amendment now prohibits takings for economic development or tax enhancement ￼. Public use in Michigan explicitly excludes taking private property for transfer to another private party for economic development ￼. Additionally, if a blight removal taking is used, each parcel’s blight must be proven and the burden of proof is clear and convincing on the agency ￼. These are among the nation’s strongest protections. In practice, this means Michigan cities/counties cannot use eminent domain for private redevelopment projects (unless truly blighted and meeting strict criteria as in County of Wayne v. Hathcock).
Minnesota	Minnesota requires a condemning authority to obtain appraisal(s) and negotiate; an owner has minimum 60 days after an offer to respond before suit. The authority must pass a resolution identifying the public use. After filing a petition, a court holds a quick hearing and, if public use/necessity is found, appoints three commissioners to assess damages. The commissioners’ award can be appealed to district court for a jury trial.	Fair market value, considering highest and best use and any assemblage or plottage if applicable. Partial takings: Minnesota is generous with minimum compensation – it has a “minimum compensation” statute ensuring homeowners or small business owners can recover enough to purchase a comparable replacement property in the community if FMV would be insufficient (Minn. Stat. §117.187). Severance damages are awarded for loss in value to remainder. No multiplier on FMV by law, but the minimum compensation rule effectively can increase the payout. Attorney fees: owner gets fees paid if the final award exceeds the last written offer by 40% (for < $50k cases) or 20% (for ≥ $50k cases) (Minn. Stat. §117.031).	Owners have the right to a court hearing on public use and necessity before commissioners are appointed. Post-Kelo law in MN removed the presumption of public use for eminent domain – the condemnor must prove public use/necessity if an owner objects, and certain proposed takings (like for economic development) face heightened scrutiny. Jury trial available on appeal of damages. Notice: at least 30 days before the court hearing, owners get served the petition and notice of hearing. Also, the initial offer must be made and the owner given time (30–60 days) to consider and obtain their own appraisal (for which the condemnor must reimburse up to $1,500).	Minnesota’s 2006 overhaul sharply curtailed eminent domain for private projects. It bans condemnation for economic development unless it’s to eliminate an actual public health or safety threat (and even then, only the problematic property can be taken). “Public use” was redefined so that public benefits of economic growth do not alone qualify. Blight takings are allowed only on a parcel that is blighted on its own (no more taking non-blighted parcels in a “blighted area” unless each one is blighted). Thus, MN virtually eliminated Kelo-type takings. Utilities and public infrastructure remain valid uses.
Mississippi	Mississippi’s procedure: a condemnor (often the Mississippi Transportation Commission or local authority) must appraise and attempt a voluntary purchase. If not successful, they file a Special Court of Eminent Domain action. Mississippi uniquely allows a quick jury trial on the necessity and compensation in the special court (which is essentially the county court or county court judge acting on eminent domain). If the condemnor needs immediate possession (e.g. highways), they can seek an order after filing by showing urgent need and posting bond.	Fair market value at highest and best use. If only part is taken, the damages to the remainder plus value of part taken equal just compensation. Mississippi does not add a percentage bonus to value. Attorney fees and costs are generally not awarded (only if the court finds the taking was not for public use, the owner might recoup costs). There is a unique provision: if a condemnor formally abandons the proceedings, it may owe the owner’s expenses. Relocation benefits as per federal law for federally funded projects; no broader state relocation statute.	Owners can demand a jury trial in the special eminent domain court proceeding (and almost always do). That jury will decide both the right to take (if the owner raises public use as a defense) and the compensation amount at the same trial. In 2011, voters approved a constitutional amendment prohibiting certain takings; now owners can cite that to block a taking that plans to transfer land to private parties for 10 years. Notice: owners get a summons to the special court and at least 30 days before the trial date. Pre-suit, they typically receive a written offer and notice that eminent domain will be filed if no agreement (no statutory period, but good practice).	Mississippi was a notable late reformer: via a 2011 constitutional amendment, Mississippi forbids transfer of taken property to private entities for 10 years after acquisition, with narrow exceptions (such as common carriers or if the property poses a public danger) ￼. This effectively prevents economic development takings – if land can’t be given to a private developer for 10 years, it removes the incentive. Prior to that, MS courts (in the 2000s) had already signaled disapproval of takings for private development. Now, public use in Mississippi means use by the public or government – taking for private enterprise is constitutionally off-limits.
Missouri	Missouri’s process strengthened after 2006 reforms: the condemnor must give at least 60 days’ notice to the owner before filing a condemnation petition ￼. A written final offer must be provided at least 30 days before filing, with explanation of how the offer was calculated ￼. After filing, the court promptly appoints three commissioners to assess damages. Their report can be paid by the condemnor to take possession (quick-take style), and either party can request a jury trial on damages afterward.	Fair market value plus substantial statutory bonuses for long-held property: If the property has been owned within the same family for ≥50 years, the owner gets 150% of fair market value as compensation ￼. If it’s the owner’s primary residence (and not covered by the 50-year rule), they receive 125% of FMV ￼. These “heritage value” bonuses apply except for takings for public utilities. Severance damages are paid for any decrease in value of remaining property. Missouri law also entitles owners to reimbursement of their reasonable attorney fees and costs if the final compensation awarded by a jury exceeds the commissioners’ award by 20% or more (RSMo §523.256).	Owners may challenge the taking’s necessity – though under Missouri law, once an ordinance or resolution declaring the project is passed, courts usually limit review to whether statutory procedures were followed ￼. Public use can be contested, but the 2006 reform created a Citizen’s right to appeal for property owners: they can appeal blight designations in court. Notice to owners is robust: 60-day advance notice of intent to file ￼, plus personal service of the petition. Owners have 30 days to file exceptions to the commissioners’ award. Jury trial available on damages. If the condemnor does not strictly follow procedures (notice periods, etc.), the taking can be voided – incentivizing compliance.	Missouri reacted to Kelo with comprehensive changes. No eminent domain for solely economic development is allowed. State law now says property cannot be condemned “solely for economic development purposes,” i.e. increased jobs/taxes ￼. They also tightened the definition of “blighted” – it must constitute a serious threat to public health/safety. Furthermore, they wrote the heritage value bonuses into law to ensure generous compensation as a deterrent to condemn marginal properties. Thus, while eminent domain is still used for infrastructure and clearing true hazards, Missouri greatly restrains any use of eminent domain that primarily benefits a private developer.
Montana	A condemnor in Montana must attempt to purchase and cannot file until 30 days after the final written offer. The condemnor also must formally adopt a resolution describing the public use and why the property is necessary. After filing in district court, a hearing is held where the court must determine if the taking is for a public use, necessary, and the offer was made. If yes, the court appoints commissioners to set compensation. Either party can appeal the award to a jury trial.	Fair market value as defined by Montana law (considering most advantageous use). Partial takings include depreciation to the remainder as damages. No statutory percentage add-ons. However, Montana does allow recovery of litigation expenses by the owner if the final award is closer to the owner’s claimed amount than the condemnor’s offer (Mont. Code §70-30-305) – essentially, if the agency undervalued significantly, it pays the owner’s fees. Relocation assistance is available under state law for displaced persons, generally mirroring federal guidelines.	Owners can contest the right to take at the preliminary hearing. Montana courts require the condemnor to show “public interest requires the taking.” Post-Kelo (2007), the law was clarified to exclude transfers to private parties as a valid public interest, giving owners a clear ground to object if it’s for economic development. Notice: the 30-day offer period is effectively notice, plus after filing, a summons is served and a hearing is set (with time for the owner to prepare objections). Jury trial right on compensation if desired.	Montana passed a law in 2007 explicitly prohibiting use of eminent domain for economic development or tax enhancement. Public use in Montana must be strictly interpreted – it generally means use by the public or by a public agency. Takings for private commercial enterprise are not allowed (except for common carrier utilities which are deemed public use). So, Montana property owners are protected from Kelo-type takings; eminent domain is limited to things like roads, public buildings, utilities, or removing public nuisances.
Nebraska	Nebraska requires condemning authorities to attempt negotiation and make a reasonable offer. If unsuccessful, they file a petition in county court. The court appoints a three-person board of appraisers (“appraisers” similar to commissioners) to inspect and award damages. Either party can appeal that award to the district court for a jury trial. No special hearing on public use is required by statute, but an owner can object in the proceeding.	Fair market value at highest use. Nebraska includes all factors a prudent buyer/seller would consider. For partial takings, pays difference in entire property value before vs. after. No statutory multipliers. Attorney fees: Neb. law (Neb. Rev Stat 76-726) gives owners their litigation costs if on appeal they obtain a award significantly higher than the initial board’s award (generally if it’s >15% higher). Also, if the condemnor abandons the taking after the board’s award, it must pay the owner’s fees. Relocation assistance provided per Nebraska Relocation Assistance Act.	Owners may file exceptions to the board’s award, which can include contesting the necessity/public use. Nebraska courts have allowed challenges if the use is clearly private or if the process wasn’t followed. Notice: besides the initial offer, after filing, each owner is served notice of proceedings. There isn’t a pre-condemnation public hearing requirement except for certain redevelopment plans (which need a blight study and hearing). Jury trial available on appeal for value.	Nebraska in 2006 enacted a law tightening definitions: it disallowed eminent domain for private economic development except to eliminate blight in an extremely blighted area (which is defined strictly). Even for blight, if it’s agricultural land outside city limits, it cannot be declared blighted for purposes of condemnation. In effect, Nebraska now bans Kelo-style takings – the property must pose a public concern (like urban blight) or be needed for traditional public ownership/use.
Nevada	Nevada dramatically changed its process via a constitutional amendment (2008). Now, before filing, the government must first provide the owner a notice of intent and a written offer. If contested, the government (except in urgent cases) must get a court determination of public use. After filing, a hearing is held where the court must find that the taking is for public use and the property is necessary. Only then can compensation be determined (by commissioners and jury trial if demanded). Quick-take is effectively curtailed by the requirement of court findings first, but for highways and utilities, possession can be obtained after necessity is found and deposit made.	Highest-compensation rule: Nevada’s Constitution (Art. 1 §22, added 2006) mandates that the property’s value reflect “the use to which it is reasonably adaptable” ￼, not just current use – ensuring owners get value for highest potential use. It also requires compensation for loss of goodwill in certain cases and precludes considering speculative reduced value from the project itself. In practice, fair market value at highest and best use is paid. If the taking is later canceled or the property not used, the condemnor may have to pay penalties. Attorney’s fees and costs are awarded to the owner if they obtain a final award more than 125% of the government’s offer (per NRS 37.180).	Owners have enhanced rights: at the necessity hearing, the condemning authority must show by clear and convincing evidence that the taking is for public use ￼ and that the property taken is necessary for that use. The owner can present evidence to rebut this. Jury trial is guaranteed for final compensation. Notice: the initial notice of intent must be sent 90 days before filing. The constitutional amendment also ensures owners receive all procedural due process, and if the property won’t be used for the stated purpose, the owner (or heir) can petition to repurchase it.	Nevada’s PISTOL Amendment (People’s Initiative to Stop the Taking of Our Land) is one of the nation’s strictest. It declares public use does not include transferring property to a private party (for any reason, including economic development). It requires “just compensation” to be broadly construed in favor of the property owner and sets procedural hurdles as noted. Essentially, no economic development takings; even blight takings are very limited (each property must present a public health/safety danger to be condemned and given to another owner). Nevada property cannot be taken to increase tax revenue or for private business—period.
New Hampshire	NH requires a vote or authorization by the governing board (or legislature for certain projects) to exercise eminent domain. An appraisal and bona fide offer are prerequisites. If proceeding, the condemnor files in the Superior Court (or in some cases, a legislative taking is done by statute). A board of commissioners may be appointed for damages, subject to appeal for jury trial.	Fair market value is paid. NH in 2006 amended its constitution to add that property value should be considered without regard to the intended use by the taker, to avoid undervaluing. No statutory percentage bonus. Severance damages allowed. No automatic fee-shifting—owners generally bear their own attorney fees unless a statute for a particular project (like pipeline) says otherwise. Relocation assistance for federally funded projects; state law has limited relocation provisions.	Owners have a constitutional right (since 2006) to challenge the taking on grounds it’s not for a public use – and the constitution now explicitly prevents judicial deference to legislative “public use” declarations if it’s for private development. Notice: owners must get notice of the vote or hearing authorizing the taking. After a petition, they get served and can contest in court. Jury trial for damages if requested.	New Hampshire’s Constitution Part I, Article 12-a (passed in 2006) clearly states: “No part of a person’s property shall be taken…if the taking is for the private use or benefit of another”. This was a direct response to Kelo. Therefore, no takings for economic development or private use are allowed in NH. Public use means public ownership or control (or use by the general public). The state has adhered to this, allowing eminent domain only for traditional uses (roads, utilities, etc.), and even tightened energy project takings (2012 law disallowing private transmission line companies from eminent domain).
New Jersey	New Jersey’s process often involves a redevelopment designation for private-project takings. Generally, the municipality must designate an “area in need of redevelopment” (blight) via planning board hearing and governing body resolution. For all takings, an appraisal and offer are required. If no agreement, a complaint is filed in Superior Court. NJ uses commissioners to determine value initially, with right to appeal to court for a jury. Quick-take (the “Declaration of Taking” act) can be used by government entities to take title at filing by depositing estimated compensation.	Fair market value standard. New Jersey allows compensation for certain tenant-owned improvements and fixtures as well. Severance damages if only part taken. After reforms, NJ enacted relocation assistance and added that for any occupant displaced from a dwelling, compensation must be sufficient to enable purchase of a comparable decent home (capping at certain limits) – effectively sometimes more than FMV for low-value homes. Attorney fees: the court may award fees if it finds the condemnor acted in bad faith or if the award is significantly above the offer (not automatic).	Owners can challenge a blight designation through an independent action – NJ courts have overturned some designations that were too broad. During condemnation, an owner can object to the taking if procedures weren’t followed, but if the area is formally designated, public use is presumed. Notice: extensive public notice and hearings are required for redevelopment designations. In direct takings (e.g. road), owners get a 14-day notice of intent to file a declaration of taking (under DOTA) and are served process. Jury trial available on appeal for value.	New Jersey made some blight reform post-Kelo. A 2007 law tightened the definition of “blighted area,” requiring specific findings (e.g. a property can’t be deemed blighted solely for being “not fully productive”). But NJ did not ban economic development takings outright – it continues to allow redevelopment condemnations, albeit under stricter standards and more public transparency. In practice, abuses have lessened, and the courts require a realistic showing of deterioration/harm. So, while NJ owners got more procedural protection, the state remains one where redevelopment takings are possible (public use can include alleviating blight or fostering redevelopment, if duly declared). Purely taking property from one private party to give to another for economic gain without a pretext of blight is generally not permitted, but with a blight designation, it can occur.
New Mexico	New Mexico requires that any eminent domain for private reuse (redevelopment) be explicitly authorized. After Kelo, the legislature imposed a moratorium on redevelopment takings. Now, eminent domain is used mainly for public facilities/infrastructure. The process: agency makes an offer, files a petition in court if not accepted, and there’s a hearing on public use. Commissioners assess damages, with right to trial de novo.	Fair market value paid. NM doesn’t have special multipliers. It follows standard valuation methods. No statutory attorney fee provision except if condemnation was not authorized (then fees may be awarded). Relocation assistance as per federal rules for projects with federal funds; state law limited.	Owners can contest public use in court and, given the 2007 reform, if it’s a private development purpose, the court will deny it. Jury trial available for compensation. Notice: owners are given a written offer and typically 30 days to consider. If filed, service of process and a court hearing schedule.	New Mexico essentially prohibited eminent domain for private economic development in 2007. The legislature passed reforms stating that municipalities cannot use eminent domain under the Metropolitan Redevelopment Act to transfer property to private developers. Public use is limited to traditional uses. Thus NM joined the majority in forbidding Kelo-type takings.
New York	New York’s procedure is governed by the Eminent Domain Procedure Law (EDPL). It requires a public hearing (for most takings) to consider the public use, benefit, and impact ￼. A Determination and Findings report must be issued within 90 days of the hearing ￼. Owners then have 30 days to challenge that determination in the appellate court. If upheld or unchallenged, the condemnor can file for acquisition. Title usually passes via court order or filing of acquisition map (for state takings) ￼. Then a separate claim for compensation is heard in the Court of Claims or Supreme Court (no commissioners; it’s a bench trial on value unless either side demands a jury – though in NY, eminent domain compensation is typically non-jury).	Fair market value in NY = highest and best use, considering all reasonable uses. NY allows consequential damages (severance) for partial takings. It does not give automatic extra percentages on value. However, NY has an “additional allowance” statute: if an owner’s compensation award is “substantially in excess” of the condemnor’s offer, the court may award the owner’s attorney, appraiser, and engineer fees ￼ ￼. In practice, about a 35% or more difference is deemed “substantially in excess” ￼. Relocation assistance is available under the EDPL for displaced owners/tenants. Trade fixtures (tenant-owned improvements) are compensated separately.	Owners have a right to participate in the EDPL hearing and submit objections. After Determination, they have 30 days to file a judicial review (Article 2 EDPL proceeding) to contest the public use, public benefit, or procedure. Courts in NY do defer to the agency’s determination if it had a rational basis, but owners have overturned takings lacking proper findings. On compensation, owners file a claim and can present valuation evidence; either party can appeal the trial result. Notice: personal notice of the EDPL hearing by mail, publication in newspapers ￼, and individual notice of the Determination with info on how to seek review. Also, owners get notice of vesting (taking) and their right to file a claim for more money.	New York notably did not enact new limits post-Kelo. It remains one of the most condemnor-friendly states. Takings for redevelopment or economic projects are allowed under broad definitions of public use (often via agencies like the Empire State Development Corp). The main requirement is compliance with EDPL procedures and a general public purpose finding. While public outrage exists, legislative reform stalled ￼. So, in NY, taking property for private development (like to eliminate “blight” or for a civic project) is legal as long as statutory processes are followed. NY courts have upheld even controversial takings (e.g. for the Atlantic Yards project in Brooklyn, finding job creation and urban renewal a valid public purpose). In short, NY continues to permit economic development takings (especially under blight designations or through public benefit corporations), setting it apart from most other states.

A condemnor (city, DOT, etc.) must adopt a resolution and provide notice to owner with an offer. If proceeding, it files a complaint and Declaration of Taking (for DOT and some others) to immediately vest title, depositing the appraised compensation. Municipalities (for non-transport projects) use a standard petition (no immediate title until judgment unless a quick-take statute applies).	Fair market value is paid. NC includes value of improvements and damages to remaining property. No bonus multipliers. If an entity abuses the process or the final award is 25% higher than the deposit, the owner may get interest and potentially litigation expenses (particularly in DOT cases under state law, owners can get atty fees if DOT’s final offer was at least 25% lower than verdict and the court finds DOT arbitrarily refused a timely settlement). Now (with the 2018 amendment), public use is narrowed to exclude direct benefits to private parties – giving owners a stronger footing to object if it’s a private development case. Jury trial is available for compensation. Notice: the owner must get at least 30 days notice before a condemnation resolution (for cities) and is served with the court filings. If a quick-take (DOT) is used, owner gets notice of deposit and 120 days to file answer contesting anything.	North Carolina was late in explicit reform; in 2018 voters approved a constitutional amendment forbidding eminent domain except for a “public use.” While “public use” is yet to be fully defined in NC courts, the intent was to prevent Kelo-style takings. Previously, NC statutes already prohibited purely private takings (and NC hadn’t engaged in high-profile redevelopment takings). With the amendment, NC aligns with others: no taking for economic development or private benefit. Takings are allowed for public infrastructure, utilities, or to eliminate unsafe structures (the Urban Redevelopment Law exists but is sparingly used and would now be interpreted strictly in light of the “public use” requirement).

A quick-take procedure is available to state and local governments: they can file a plat and deposit in court, which immediately transfers title (after serving notice to owner). A relatively unique aspect: Ohio often bifurcates the proceeding – first a hearing on the right to take (if the owner objects), then, if allowed, a valuation trial. Ohio did not adopt multipliers, but its courts have sometimes recognized that in wrongful takings owners might get additional remedies (though typically takings are just voided, not paid extra). Severance damages included. Attorney fees: Ohio generally awards them if the agency is found not to have the right to take (wrongful taking). Oklahoma	Oklahoma typically requires the condemning entity to pass a resolution and attempt negotiations. Then it files a petition in district court. Oklahoma’s Supreme Court effectively forbade Kelo-type takings by constitutional interpretation ￼, and the legislature reinforced it in 2006 by defining public use narrowly and banning taking for private economic development. Oregon	Oregon’s eminent domain process is administrative followed by judicial if needed. Government must give at least 40 days’ notice to owner with an offer and appraisal. If refused, the condemnor adopts a resolution and files a complaint. Oregon does not use commissioner panels; it goes straight to a jury trial for compensation, unless parties settle.	Fair market value at highest use. After Measure 39 (2006), if property is taken and later not used for stated public use, the former owner can claim compensation. No added percentage on FMV by statute. Attorney fees: Oregon has a “threshold” rule – if the final judgment exceeds the condemnor’s highest written offer pre-trial by 30% or more, the owner is entitled to attorney fees (ORS 35.300). Oregon voters passed Measure 39 (2006), which prohibits public bodies from using eminent domain to transfer property to another private party. Exceptions exist for public utilities and common carriers. This means OR bars economic development takings and most redevelopments – a property taken must generally remain in public ownership or use. Fair market value, considering all permissible uses. Notably, PA exempted a few big cities’ existing redevelopment areas temporarily, but those exemptions expired. Rhode Island	Rhode Island’s process is governed by Title 37 of state law. A condemnor (state agency) can file a declaration of taking in the land evidence records, which immediately transfers title, and must notify the owner. An appraisal and offer should precede that if practical. The owner can then claim additional damages in Superior Court if dissatisfied. For municipal takings (like redevelopment), a similar approach: pass an order of taking, record it, notify owner, then handle damages via court if contested.	Fair market value. RI includes all direct damages and severance damages. No special enhancement or multiplier. Attorney fees are generally not awarded unless the taking was illegal. Relocation assistance in line with federal guidelines for any state projects (Rhode Island has adopted the federal Uniform Relocation Act by reference).	Owners can challenge a taking’s validity via an injunction or in the damages case (arguing no public use). Historically, RI had few outright bans on economic development takings, but such takings have been infrequent. Owners get notice after the taking (which is a drawback – title can vest without prior notice, though agencies often inform owners before recording to negotiate). Once notified, an owner has 3 months to file a notice of appeal for more compensation, and 1 year to actually file the lawsuit. A trial (bench trial typically) will decide compensation.	Rhode Island did not pass new legislation post-Kelo. The state’s use of eminent domain is mostly for infrastructure; Providence’s past redevelopment projects (like 1960s “Capital Center”) did involve takings, but nowadays political climate deters pure private transfer takings. However, there’s no explicit statutory prohibition – it’s just guided by federal constitutional limits. So, while de facto RI avoids Kelo-style condemnations, de jure a city or the state could still condemn blighted areas for redevelopment. Any such action would have to clear the state’s constitutional “public use” requirement (courts here would likely strike a taking that solely aims to confer a private benefit).
South Carolina	South Carolina’s procedures require a condemnor to provide at least 30 days’ notice to the owner with a description of the project and offer. After that, they file a Condemnation Notice (in probate court for some entities, circuit court for others). SC often allows “quick take” – the notice of condemnation and deposit of compensation vests title on a specified date. The owner can then contest or appeal compensation.	Fair market value. SC’s constitution was amended in 2006 to say the General Assembly must define public use and that just compensation for condemning a home must be no less than the fair market value of the property’s highest and best use. Thus, even if a home is valued higher as commercial, that must be considered. Severance damages included. No extra percentage but highest use requirement boosts value. Attorney fees: if the final verdict exceeds the condemnor’s offer by 15%+, the owner gets fees (SC Code §28-2-510).	Owners can request a trial in circuit court for compensation (the initial proceeding might be before a judge alone). Public use challenges are permitted; the 2006 amendment explicitly disallowed takings for private benefit, so owners have that constitutional protection. Notice: 30-day notice of intent and offer. After condemnation, owner has 30 days to file an answer contesting anything (public use or adequacy of offer). Jury trial if requested on value.	South Carolina’s constitutional amendment (Art I §13) in 2006 forbids eminent domain for private use and specifically states benefit to economic development does not constitute a public use. It also tightened that blighted property can only be condemned if it causes a public harm (and even then, not for the purpose of later transferring to a private party). Thus, SC prohibits Kelo-type takings. Public use must be literal (roads, public facilities, etc., or true nuisance abatement).
South Dakota	South Dakota moved quickly in 2006 to ban private-party takings. Procedure-wise, a condemnor must negotiate and then apply to circuit court. A hearing on necessity may be held. If approved, court appoints commissioners to award compensation. Either side can appeal to a jury trial.	Fair market value. No special multiplier. Severance damages allowed if applicable. SD does not generally award attorney fees except in inverse condemnation. It does mandate that if the verdict exceeds the offer by >20%, the court shall award the owner’s appraisal costs (but not necessarily full legal fees).	Owners can contest the taking on public use. SD statute (updated in 2006) now puts the burden on condemnors to prove public use and necessity. Jury trial on compensation if desired. Notice: requirement of a 30-day negotiation period; notice of hearing if one is held for necessity (not always required).	South Dakota law now states that property cannot be taken for the purpose of transferring to any private person, nongovernmental entity, or for enhancing tax revenues. In short, economic development is off the table. Public use is limited to traditional uses and blight takings (and SD even restricted blight – they repealed a statute that had allowed takings for blight/urban renewal). So SD’s stance is among the strictest: eminent domain only for clear public ownership/use.
Tennessee	Tennessee requires condemners to attempt a negotiated purchase and give the owner an opportunity (30 days) to consider. If filing is needed, the condemner files a petition in court. The court will determine public use/necessity (if contested) and then, typically, assign the matter to a jury of view (commission) to report damages. The jury of view’s award can be appealed to a new trial before a judge and jury. (In some cases, especially DOT, a direct court valuation without jury of view is done.) Quick-take is allowed for DOT: they can take possession 30 days after filing by depositing funds.	Fair market value at highest and best use. Tennessee enacted a “cost reimbursement” statute: if an owner’s property is taken for public use, the condemner must reimburse up to $10,000 of the owner’s expenses (including attorney fees) regardless of outcome (Tenn. Code 29-17-106). Additionally, if the final award is ≥ the condemner’s offer by 20%+, the owner gets full attorneys’ fees paid. Severance damages included for remainder. No statutory multipliers.	Owners have the right to a court determination of public use. Post-2006 changes in TN law demand clear evidence of public use when an owner objects, and certain condemnations (for industrial parks, etc.) now require blight conditions. Notice: at least 30 days before filing, final offer must be made. If filed, owner is served and can answer contesting public use or necessity. A relatively quick hearing on that can be requested. Jury trial on value at some stage (jury of view then jury, or directly jury) is assured.	Tennessee’s 2006 eminent domain reform barred use of eminent domain for private development. The law now says “public use” shall not include either private use or benefit, or the sake of tax revenue or economic development. There are exceptions for public utilities and common carriers. Takings for blight are permitted but only for properties that meet a narrow definition of blight (and eminent domain cannot be used to acquire non-blighted properties in a blighted area). Thus, TN does not allow eminent domain simply to hand property to a private developer or boost the economy.
Texas	Texas has one of the most detailed processes (see Landowner’s Bill of Rights). An entity with condemnation power must: provide the owner a Landowner’s Bill of Rights document explaining the process ￼; make an initial bona fide offer including an appraisal ￼; wait at least 30 days; then make a final offer in writing (including a copy of a third-party appraisal) ￼; wait 14 days after final offer ￼; and if still no agreement, file a petition in court. The court appoints three local landowners as special commissioners to hold a hearing and determine compensation. Either party can object to the commissioners’ award, which leads to a de novo trial in county court. During the process, the condemnor can deposit the awarded amount and take possession after the commissioners’ award, even if objections are filed (immediate possession upon deposit).	Fair market value (“adequate compensation”) is required by constitution, defined as market value of the property taken plus damage to any remaining property ￼. Texas law explicitly includes injury to the remainder in compensation ￼. It does not compensate subjective values or business losses (unless they affect real property value), but it does pay for some relocation expenses (separate from eminent domain damages, via the Relocation Assistance Act). Attorney fees are not awarded in a typical case where the taking is valid (each party pays their own), except if the condemnor is found to lack authority or fails procedural requirements, then the owner can get costs. (Texas in 2011 strengthened that if an entity abuses the process or condemns without proper offer, it may have to pay owner’s fees.)	Owners’ rights are strongly protected by statute: they receive a detailed Bill of Rights informing them of the right to compensation, right to negotiate, right to a hearing, and right to a jury trial. They can hire their own appraiser and attorney. Texas does allow contesting the right to condemn (e.g. if not truly for public use or the entity lacks authority), which the court will hear – though after 2009 reforms and a 2015 constitutional amendment, public use is narrowly defined, so owners have a clear path to win if the purpose is suspect. Notice periods are built-in: 45 days minimum from first offer to final offer, and 14+ days after final offer before filing ￼ ￼ ￼. Jury trial is available if either party objects to the commissioners’ award – the case proceeds as normal civil trial on value.	Texas’ law now explicitly states no eminent domain for private use or for economic development (with narrow exceptions) ￼. The Texas Government and Property Codes were amended (2005, 2007, 2011) to tighten public use: a taking cannot be for economic development or tax enhancement ￼. Even blight takings were limited – Texas eliminated the ability of cities to blight designate an area just for economic development; now, condemnation for blight is mostly limited to removing actual urban decay that poses a hazard. Additionally, a 2009 constitutional amendment (Art I §17) entrenched these rules, making Texas one of the most protective states for landowners. In summary, unless it’s for a recognized public use (roads, schools, utilities, etc.), Texas condemning entities cannot take property simply to hand it to a private developer.
Utah	A condemnor in Utah must provide the owner with notice of intent and a written offer at least 30 days before filing (Utah Code §78B-6-505). The governing body of the entity must adopt a resolution declaring the public use and that condemnation is necessary. Once filed, the court action proceeds; Utah does not use commissioner panels – it goes to a trial (bench or jury) on compensation if needed. Possession can be obtained before final judgment by depositing estimated compensation and upon court order.	Fair market value at highest and best use. Severance damages for partial takings are included. No additional percentage by law. If the final award by jury exceeds the condemnor’s offer by more than 10%, the court will award the owner’s litigation expenses (Utah Code §78B-6-509). Utah also has a relocation assistance statute ensuring payment of moving costs to displaced persons, not as part of condemnation damages but as a separate obligation.	Owners may challenge the taking in court; Utah in 2007 clarified public uses in statute, effectively disallowing pure private benefit takings. The owner can request a hearing on public use/necessity, though typically that’s decided by the resolution (which courts review for arbitrariness). Jury trial is permitted on compensation. Notice: 30-day prior notice of intent/offer is required, and a copy of the authorizing resolution should be provided.	Utah enacted legislation in 2007 stating that eminent domain cannot be used for solely economic development (Utah Code §78B-6-501(3) declares that increasing tax base, tax revenues or employment is not by itself a public use). Also, Utah outlawed use of eminent domain for recreational trails across private property. So, Utah aligns with the post-Kelo consensus: no taking for private development purposes. Public use must be genuine – e.g., roads, utilities, or remedying immediate public dangers.
Vermont	Vermont’s eminent domain usage is relatively infrequent. When used (often for highways or utilities), the agency must appraise and negotiate. If needed, the agency (e.g. VTrans) issues a necessity petition to the court or a state board. VT often has a Necessity phase – a hearing (sometimes by a state Transportation Board or court) to decide if the project is necessary and in the public interest. If yes, then damages are either negotiated or decided by commissioners and a Superior Court process.	Fair market value standard. Vermont doesn’t offer extra percentage bonuses. It does ensure compensation for farm or forest land includes any agricultural value if higher than market (to mitigate unique use loss). Attorney fees are not routinely awarded (only if statute for a specific project allows or if the taking was wrongful).	Owners can participate in the necessity hearing – Vermont is one of few that consider public interest/necessity in a quasi-judicial process explicitly. They can appeal a necessity decision to Superior Court. Jury trial is available on damages. Notice: owners get notice of the necessity petition and hearing, and later notice of valuation proceedings.	Vermont did not pass a new law post-Kelo – partly due to low incidence of commercial redevelopment takings. By policy, VT authorities avoid private-use takings. There is no statutory allowance for takings for economic development, so arguably it wasn’t an issue. Practically, Vermont would not approve a taking for private development absent a clear public oversight or purpose (like perhaps a utility or public-private partnership with strong public control). While no explicit ban exists in writing, any attempted Kelo-like taking would likely be struck down under the state’s traditional public use doctrine and public backlash.
Virginia	Virginia requires a formal certificate of take or court petition. Post-2012 reforms, any condemnor must issue a written offer 30 days before taking action and conduct a bona fide negotiation. If proceeding, for most takings, they file a certificate with the court and deposit funds (quick-take) – title and possession then vest in the condemnor. The owner then has 21 days to request a court to review necessity if they believe excess property was taken, and 90 days to appeal the amount of compensation (if no agreement) to a jury.	Virginia’s Constitution (Art. I §11) now demands owners be paid just compensation which explicitly includes the value of lost profits and lost access, as applicable ￼ ￼. By statute, business owners can claim lost profits for up to 3 years (with proof, for businesses on the taken property) and property owners can claim value reduction due to loss of access to roads ￼. These are in addition to fair market value of the property. “Fair market value” is highest price for the property in an open market ￼. Severance damages are included (diminution in value of remainder). Attorney fees: if a condemnor doesn’t strictly follow procedures or abuses the process, the court can award fees; also if an inverse condemnation suit by owner succeeds, fees are awarded. Generally, in a direct taking with proper procedure, each side pays own fees (no automatic fee-shifting for low offers in VA).	Owners can challenge the taking on grounds of lack of public use or excess taking. VA’s constitutional amendment removed the presumption in favor of governments on public use – owners can have a court independently judge if the stated use fits the narrow definition ￼ ￼. Owners also may argue that more land than necessary is taken, in which case the court can invalidate the excess ￼. Notice: at least 30 days before filing a certificate, the condemnor must give the offer and a statement of the public use. Post-filing, owners get notice of their rights to challenge and the timeline. A commission or jury will determine compensation unless waived.	Virginia’s 2012 constitutional amendment and enabling laws radically tightened eminent domain. No taking for private gain, private benefit, private enterprise, increasing jobs, or tax revenue is permitted ￼. Public use is limited to ownership/occupation by public or for public utility/transportation facilities, or to eliminate a property that is a public safety danger (blight) ￼. Even blight condemnation can’t be used to acquire non-blighted property for private development – and “blight” itself in VA is defined narrowly (property that endangers public health/safety). Also, taking more property than needed (excess acreage) is prohibited. In sum, VA virtually prohibits all Kelo-type takings, and it even built in compensation for lost profits/access to ensure owners are fully indemnified when a taking does occur ￼ ￼.
Washington	Washington procedure: a condemnor must pass an ordinance or resolution declaring the public use and necessity. The owner gets notice of a court petition for an “Order Adjudicating Public Use and Necessity.” The court hearing will confirm public use/necessity (owners can contest). Once that order is entered, the matter goes to a compensation trial (often before a jury). Immediate possession can be sought after the public use order, upon deposit of funds and court approval.	Fair market value at highest and best use. WA allows compensation for improvements and fixtures taken. Severance damages for remainder are allowed, and any special benefits (value increase specifically due to the project) can offset those damages (common in partial takings in WA). No multipliers. Attorney fees: Washington will award attorney fees and costs to the owner if the final compensation awarded at trial exceeds the condemnor’s highest offer by 10% or more (RCW 8.25.070).	Owners can oppose the public use in the initial hearing; post-Kelo, courts are a bit more receptive, but generally if a project is authorized and ostensibly public, the threshold is low. However, WA passed some constraints on redevelopment takings in 2007: now, to condemn property for private redevelopment, it must be blighted and a finding to that effect must be made – owners can dispute blight at the public use hearing. Notice: owners are served with the petition and hearing date for public use, typically a 20-day notice. After the order, a trial for compensation is scheduled. Jury trial is a right on compensation.	The Washington legislature didn’t amend the state constitution but did pass laws in 2007 to curb eminent domain abuse. Now, eminent domain cannot be used for economic development unless it is to acquire blighted property and only that property (can’t take non-blighted parcel just because it’s in a blighted area). They also barred takings for private use under the definition of public use. So, effectively, WA disallows Kelo-style takings (it had one of the highest-profile failed initiatives in 2006, but then legislated a compromise). Public use is back to traditional meanings, plus genuine blight removal.
West Virginia	West Virginia procedure: a condemnor files a petition in circuit court, and may request a quick-take (by filing a bond and depositing the appraised amount, it can take possession after 30 days). Commissioners are appointed to determine value; either party can appeal for a jury trial. WV also permits an alternative “petition for determination of right to take” if the owner challenges public use, to be resolved early.	Fair market value at highest and best use. WV allows consideration of replacement cost for certain facilities if no market. Severance damages for partial takings. No statutory bonus, but if the jury award is 10% higher than the condemnor’s final offer, the owner gets litigation costs and attorney fees (W.Va. Code §54-2-13).	Owners can file a response denying public use; WV courts (even before Kelo) had stated that public use does not include taking for private economic development, and a 2006 law codified that. So owners have strong ground to stop an invalid taking. Jury trial available for value. Notice: typically, the owner is contacted and offered compensation; after filing, a notice of hearing on quick-take (if sought) and the case timeline is given.	West Virginia enacted a law in 2006 saying property may not be condemned for private economic development (Code 54-1-2). Combined with prior court rulings, WV now forbids using eminent domain to transfer property to another private entity for economic gain. Public use is limited to things like infrastructure, public facilities, and removal of structure posing danger (blight, narrowly defined).
Wisconsin	Wisconsin requires an initial negotiation and a jurisdictional offer in writing. If the owner rejects or does not respond, the condemnor can issue an Award of Damages (essentially an administrative taking) and file it with the county – title then transfers to the condemnor. The owner then has 2 years to appeal the compensation to the circuit court (where a jury trial is available). In some cases (if statutory authority requires), a court petition is used instead, but generally WI uses the administrative “award” method.	Fair market value, considering highest and best use. In 2006, WI created extra protections: if a person’s primary residence is taken for a highway project, they may elect either FMV or a replacement home of equivalent utility (within certain cost limits) paid by the agency. No general multiplier though. Severance damages included. Litigation expenses are awarded to the owner if the jury verdict exceeds the condemnor’s offer by 15% or more and $700 (Wis. Stat. §32.28).	Owners can challenge the right to take via a separate injunction action (must be done within 40 days of the jurisdictional offer). Wisconsin’s 2006 law prohibits condemnation for private development, so an owner could argue lack of public use if that is the case. Jury trial for compensation if appealed. Notice: a jurisdictional offer must be delivered at least 20 days before making an award of taking. Owners get a full statement of rights. If they don’t accept the offer, the taking happens but they can still appeal compensation.	Wisconsin’s 2006 Act 233 prohibited eminent domain for private economic development unless it is to eliminate blight (and blight is defined more strictly). Even for blight, owner-occupied residences cannot be taken under blight unless they are a danger to health/safety. So WI largely banned Kelo-type uses. Public use = public ownership or truly public purpose. Transferring acquired land to a private entity is disfavored except for very narrow cases (like utilities).
Wyoming	A condemnor in Wyoming must attempt to purchase and if failing, file a petition in district court. A commissioners’ panel of 3 freeholders is appointed to evaluate compensation. Either side can appeal to a jury trial. Quick-take is allowed for the state in certain cases: they can take possession after petition if urgent (with court approval and bond).	Fair market value standard. WY has no special enhancements. It does allow recovery of attorney fees for owners if the commissioners or jury award is more than the condemnor’s offer by a certain percentage (usually significant difference required).	Owners may contest the taking; Wyoming in 2007 passed a law forbidding takings for transfer to private parties, giving owners that defense. Jury trial on appeal for value. Notice: a final offer must be given at least 30 days before filing. After filing, standard civil notice and time to object.	Wyoming’s post-Kelo statute (2007) states that ** eminent domain shall not be used to take private property to acquire it for transfer to another private party** for economic development. There are exceptions for common carriers and public utilities. So Wyoming joined the majority in disallowing purely private benefit condemnations. Public use must be direct or a recognized public necessity (roads, pipelines, etc.), and even then the law emphasizes minimal takings.
